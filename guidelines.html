Overview

In-Situ had three subsidiaries and multiple contributors producing documentation and software copy — with no shared standards. Inconsistency was growing. I designed, socialized, and maintained a content governance system that established shared standards across the entire organization, mapped to existing UX principles to accelerate buy-in. It's still in active use today.

Process

01

Designing the governance framework

I mapped writing standards — vocabulary, grammar, style, and tone — directly to In-Situ's five existing UX principles, using a voice chart as the organizing artifact. Anchoring the framework to principles people already owned gave it immediate credibility and removed the "who asked for this?" friction that kills most style guides before they launch.

02

Socializing across the organization

A governance system no one knows about is just a document. I used sprint demo sessions — attended by engineers, designers, managers, and writers across all three subsidiaries — to introduce the standards and gather feedback. I treated the launch as a change management moment, not a publishing event. The response was immediate buy-in, not resistance.

03

Embedding governance into every launch

Standards only persist if they're practiced visibly. I incorporated the guidelines into every manual and interface copy I produced, then called it out explicitly at sprint demos — showing the team how governance translated into real decisions. Over time, the system became internalized rather than enforced. In-Situ still uses and maintains the guidelines today, without me in the room.

Why It Lasted

Most governance frameworks fail because they're treated as writing projects, not change management projects. This one succeeded because the framework was anchored to existing beliefs, the launch was treated as an organizational moment, and adoption was reinforced continuously — not announced once and abandoned. That's the difference between a document and a system.

Customers have called In-Situ's documentation the best they've ever seen. The writing guidelines are a key part of why.
